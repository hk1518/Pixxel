Spatial Resolution.

Refers to the clarity of an image. A spatial resolution of 1m means, the sensor can identify/distinguish images/ objects 1m apart. 

Sure! Let's consider an example where the GSD value and the spatial resolution
 value are different:

Example Scenario:
We have an aerial image captured by a drone that covers an area of 500 meters
in width and 300 meters in height. The image has a resolution of 1000 pixels 
in width and 600 pixels in height.

GSD Calculation:
To calculate the GSD, we divide the size of the ground area covered by the 
width or height of the image in pixels.

GSD (width) = Width of the ground area / Number of pixels in width
GSD (height) = Height of the ground area / Number of pixels in height

GSD (width) = 500 meters / 1000 pixels = 0.5 meters/pixel
GSD (height) = 300 meters / 600 pixels = 0.5 meters/pixel

Spatial Resolution:
The spatial resolution refers to the ability to distinguish between two 
separate points or objects in an image. In this example, the spatial resolution is 
0.5 meters/pixel in both the width and height directions.

Difference between GSD and Spatial Resolution:
In this example, both the GSD and the spatial resolution are the same value of 
0.5 meters/pixel. The GSD tells us that each pixel in the image represents an 
area of 0.5 square meters on the ground. The spatial resolution of 0.5 meters/pixel 
indicates the level of detail that the sensor can capture.

However, it's important to note that GSD and spatial resolution can differ in 
other cases. For example, in satellite imagery, the spatial resolution might 
be specified as 1 meter/pixel, but the GSD might vary depending on the 
satellite's altitude, sensor specifications, and ground area covered by 
each pixel.

In summary, GSD and spatial resolution are related concepts, and in some cases, 
they can have the same value, as seen in the example above. However, 
they can differ based on specific imaging systems and applications. 
GSD specifically quantifies the physical size of one pixel on the ground, 
while spatial resolution indicates the level of detail that the sensor can capture.

Difference between Vector & Rastor data.

Vector Data: Imagine you have a map, and you want to show different places on it, like cities, rivers, and boundaries. In vector data, you use points, lines, and polygons to represent these features.

  • Points: Think of a point as a tiny dot on the map representing a specific location, like a city's exact position.
  • Lines: Lines are like thin strings connecting points on the map. They can show things like roads or rivers.
  • Polygons: Picture a polygon as a closed shape on the map, like the borders of a country or the outline of a lake.

Each feature (point, line, or polygon) on the map also has extra information, like the name of the city, the length of a road, or the area of a lake. This extra information is the attributes of the feature.

Raster Data: Now, think of a map made up of tiny squares (pixels) like the pixels on your computer screen or a digital photo.

Each square represents a small area on the map. In raster data, you use these pixels to show information about the entire area.

For example, each pixel might represent the temperature of that area, the elevation (height) of the land, or the color of the landscape in a satellite image.

Unlike vector data, where you represent individual features, raster data shows information continuously across the map. It's like painting the entire map with different colors to show various characteristics of the land.

In Summary:

  • Vector Data: Uses points, lines, and polygons to represent specific features on the map, each with its own attributes (like city names or road lengths).
  • Raster Data: Divides the map into tiny squares (pixels) and shows continuous information across the whole area (like temperature, elevation, or colors in an image).

Both types of data are essential in geographic information systems (GIS) for different purposes, and they help us understand and analyze the world around us in various ways.

Synthetic Aperture Radar

A remote sensing technology that sends microwaves signals toward the earth and they are reflected back to the sensor, which then processes the image with the received signal. Signal can pass through clouds, and get data even in bad weather. Used in land degradation, mining, etc.
